# Executive Advisor Memo — /executive Section
**Date:** 2026-07-23
**Reviewed:** `/executive` landing + 4 sub-pages (Leadership Philosophy, 90-Day Plan, SWOT,
Interview Positioning), cross-checked against `/resume`.

## Bottom line

This section was built specifically to answer a recruiter's complaint that the portfolio shows
*what* Dustin built but not *how* he thinks. It closes part of that gap — the writing is at the
right altitude, and the SWOT page in particular has a candor that most self-authored executive
pitches lack. But held to the standard of surviving a skeptical exec's "why" — not "what did you
believe" but "walk me through the actual decision" — three of the four pages currently read as
**assertions and frameworks, not evidence**. There is exactly one structural gap that explains
most of the weakness: none of the four pages narrates a single concrete, high-stakes, ambiguous
decision start to finish (situation → options considered → tradeoff made → outcome). Everything
is a principle with a two-word example fragment bolted on, never the story itself. That's what a
skeptical exec will pull on first, and right now there's nothing to hand them.

## Where it demonstrates real judgment

- **SWOT — Threats quadrant.** Naming the risk of "being pigeonholed as a MarTech or
  data-platform-only PM" and the risk that "title tenure alone can understate actual seniority"
  is the most convincing writing in the section. Most candidate-authored self-assessments never
  admit market-perception risk about themselves; doing so unprompted reads as someone who has
  actually sat across from a hiring committee and absorbed the objection, not someone reciting a
  personal-brand script. This is the one place the content would hold up in a live conversation
  with no follow-up needed.
- **Interview Positioning — prep-row framing.** Anticipating "You've held Principal/Director
  titles, not VP — why consider you for this scope?" and answering with scope carried ($7M+ P&L,
  9 direct reports, repeated 0→1 initiatives) rather than re-litigating the title is the right
  instinct — it reframes the question instead of getting defensive. Same for the MarTech
  question: naming "customer data is the common thread, not MarTech specifically" is a real
  reframe, not a dodge.
- **90-Day Plan — sequencing logic.** "Hold off on recommendations. This phase is about
  listening, not fixing" in the Days 1–30 bullets is a small but real signal — it's the one line
  in the plan that shows restraint as a deliberate choice rather than eagerness to look busy in
  week one.

## Where it reads as execution-only or generic

- **90-Day Plan overall.** "Absorb broadly, prioritize aggressively, then deliver and prove it"
  is the standard 30-60-90 template shape almost any competent PM would submit. Nothing in it is
  wrong, but nothing in it is falsifiable to this candidate specifically — swap the name and it
  reads the same. A skeptical exec has seen dozens of these; the way to survive the probe is one
  sentence that only someone who has actually run this play could write (a specific failure mode
  he watches for, a specific first-30-days mistake he's seen other leaders make). As written,
  it's a template, not a track record.
- **Leadership Philosophy — repeated slogan without a decision attached.** "Bring business
  clarity to technical ambiguity" appears near-verbatim on this page *and* in the resume intro.
  Repetition across audiences is intentional (the Interview Positioning page says the goal is a
  narrative that "holds together across an entire interview process"), but the risk cuts the
  other way in a live conversation: if the same sentence surfaces three times without a story
  behind it, it reads as a rehearsed talking point rather than a genuinely internalized belief.
  Every principle here ends in a project name ("database production planning to AI-native product
  operations," "Product Catalog," "the AI product operating system") but never unpacks what the
  ambiguity actually was, what the alternative path was, or why one was chosen over the other.
  Compare "Earn trust through evidence, not promises... deterministic before AI-assisted, paper
  trading before real money" — this is the closest the page gets to a real tradeoff, and it's
  still a fragment, not a story.
- **Interview Positioning — "say no" prep row has no example loaded.** The angle for "Tell me
  about a time you had to say no to something important" is "aggressive prioritization is core to
  how I operate" — which restates the question's premise back as a principle instead of answering
  it. This is exactly the question where an exec expects a specific instance (what was proposed,
  who wanted it, what was said no to, what happened after). As written, this prep row would fail
  live — it's a reminder to have an answer, not the answer.
- **SWOT — Weaknesses are the two safest admissions in the genre.** "Can get pulled too deep into
  technical detail" and "have to consciously work at staying proactive" are honestly stated
  (no humble-brag spin), which is a point in their favor. But they're also the two most
  commonly-cited "weaknesses" in executive interviews generally — a sharper move would be one
  genuine miscalibration: a decision that turned out wrong and what changed afterward. Right now
  the Threats quadrant carries more real self-awareness than the Weaknesses quadrant does, which
  is backwards — weaknesses should be where the personal accountability shows up most, not the
  market-positioning quadrant.
- **No woven citation back to `/work`.** The landing page correctly separates audiences ("these
  four artifacts... not what I've shipped, but how I think"), but then none of the four pages
  links a specific principle or pillar to the case study that actually proves it inline. "Start
  with the use case, not the solution" would land harder as a claim if it linked directly to the
  Product Catalog or AIM case study at the point it's asserted, rather than leaving the reader to
  go find the receipts themselves in a different section of the site. This is a structural,
  cheaply-fixable gap, not a tone problem.

## Would this survive five minutes with a skeptical exec?

Two of the four pages would (SWOT, and the reframing moves in Interview Positioning). The other
two — Leadership Philosophy and the 90-Day Plan — would prompt an immediate "give me a specific
example" that the current content isn't equipped to answer, because the site states the principle
but not the decision. The fix isn't more principles or more polish on the prose (the prose
altitude is already right); it's adding, per page, one real story with a beginning, a competing
option that was rejected, and a reason — ideally the same ones already implied by the fragments
(the Astronomer replacement, the executive mobile app 0→1 call, the "say no" instance) but told
end to end instead of dropped as a parenthetical.
