# Technical Peer Review — Executive Positioning Materials

**Reviewer lens:** engineering lead / staff engineer evaluating whether technical claims hold up under a "walk me through how you actually built X" follow-up.
**Scope:** `src/components/resume/content.ts`, `src/components/executive/leadership-philosophy/content.ts`, `src/components/executive/swot/content.ts`, `src/components/executive/interview-positioning/content.ts`, cross-checked against `src/lib/work.ts` and the actual case-study implementations (`src/components/pulse/`, `src/components/catalog/`, `src/components/opsystem/`, `src/components/reqon/`).
**Date:** 2026-07-23

## Overall read

The case studies backing the mobile app (Executive Companion Pulse), Product Catalog, and AI Product Operating System are genuinely well-grounded — the resume bullets for those three initiatives match the stack, scope, and role framing shown in the actual case-study pages almost exactly (React Native/TypeScript/Okta/LaunchDarkly/DataDog; React/TypeScript/GraphQL; Claude Skills/Jira/Confluence). A technical interviewer who reads the resume and then clicks through to the case study would find the story holds together.

The problem is the other half of the Acoustic bullet list — the Snowflake/data-platform/FinOps/monetization work — which has **no case study, no interactive recreation, and no supporting detail anywhere on the site**. Those bullets are exactly the kind of enterprise-PM claim ("led replacement of a vendor with homegrown orchestration," "FinOps program for cost governance") that reads as buzzword assembly to a technical reader specifically *because* nothing else on the site demonstrates the mechanics. The asymmetry is the core finding: three claims are demonstrably real, three are asserted with zero evidence trail, and they sit in the same bullet list under a "Stay technically hands-on" narrative that implies uniform depth.

## Findings

### 1. "MCP integrations" is a name-drop unsupported by the case study it's supposed to summarize
`src/components/resume/content.ts:49`: *"Designed and institutionalized an AI-native product operating system of reusable Claude Code skills, **MCP integrations**, and agentic workflows."*

The actual case study (`src/components/opsystem/content.ts`) never mentions MCP anywhere — its stack badge says "Claude Skills + Jira + Confluence," and `roleTechnical` describes the integration as "Connected to internal company-context and customer-intelligence systems for automatic enrichment," with no protocol named. `MCP` appears literally nowhere else in `src/` except this one resume bullet. A technical interviewer who has read the case study page (which the resume links to) will notice the resume claims a specific protocol the case study doesn't back up, and the natural follow-up — "which MCP servers, connecting to what, doing what" — has no answer in the material. Either name the actual MCP servers/tools in the case study's `roleTechnical` list, or drop "MCP integrations" from the resume bullet so the two pieces of content agree.

### 2. The Snowflake/Astronomer bullet has no mechanism — it's a result with the "how" removed
`src/components/resume/content.ts:50`: *"Led replacement of a ~$200K/year Astronomer dependency with homegrown Snowflake-native orchestration, eliminating vendor spend with zero delivery disruption."*

"Snowflake-native orchestration" could mean Snowflake Tasks + Streams, a Snowpark-based scheduler, dbt Cloud jobs, or a hand-rolled cron+stored-procedure system — these are meaningfully different engineering decisions with different tradeoffs (DAG expressiveness, retry/backfill semantics, observability), and a technical interviewer will ask which one and why. As written, the bullet is unfalsifiable and reads as a business-case summary (vendor cost eliminated) wearing technical vocabulary, not a technical claim. There's no case study, diagram, or even a single supporting sentence anywhere else on the site the way there is for the mobile app or the AI ops system. Recommend either (a) naming the actual orchestration mechanism in one added clause, or (b) reframing the bullet honestly as a vendor-consolidation/cost decision led rather than an engineering build, since "Led replacement... with homegrown ... orchestration" doesn't actually claim Dustin wrote the orchestration — but it's positioned next to "stay hands-on" language that implies he did.

### 3. The FinOps/data-lake bullet stacks three distinct initiatives into one line with no specifics on any of them
`src/components/resume/content.ts:51`: *"Launched a Snowflake Marketplace customer intelligence data product and led migration to a shared enterprise data lake with a Snowflake FinOps program for cost governance."*

Three separate claims compressed into one bullet: (1) shipped a Snowflake Marketplace listing, (2) led a data-lake migration, (3) built a "FinOps program." "FinOps program for cost governance" is the phrase most likely to draw a skeptical follow-up — real Snowflake FinOps work has a specific shape (warehouse right-sizing, query-cost attribution/chargeback, auto-suspend tuning, resource monitors with budget alerts) and the bullet gives none of it. Compare to the mobile-app bullet, which names the actual tools (Okta, LaunchDarkly, DataDog) rather than the category of work ("observability program"). Tightening this to even one concrete mechanism (e.g. "resource monitors + warehouse-level chargeback dashboards") would make it defensible the same way the mobile app bullet is.

### 4. "Auditable, automated reconciliation platform" doesn't say who built it or with what
`src/components/resume/content.ts:52`: *"Replaced a fragile, single-person manual monetization process with an auditable, automated reconciliation platform, eliminating a key-person risk."*

No stack, no scale ("reconciling what, how many transactions, what granularity of audit trail"), no owner-verb ("built" vs. "directed a build" vs. "specified"). This is the vaguest bullet in the list — it would survive a non-technical reader without objection but is the first one a technical interviewer would probe, and there's currently nothing to say beyond what's already written.

### 5. "Stay technically hands-on" / "I don't hand off understanding to others" is stated as a universal, but the evidence trail only covers half the portfolio
`src/components/executive/leadership-philosophy/content.ts:22-24` (Principle: "Stay technically hands-on" — *"I don't hand off understanding to others. I prototype, build, and go deep enough to have an independent, defensible opinion"*) and `src/components/executive/swot/content.ts:18` (Strength: *"Deeply hands-on — prototype and build real, working software rather than relying on others to translate ideas, so the technical credibility isn't secondhand"*) and `src/components/executive/interview-positioning/content.ts:12-14` (Pillar: *"Technical fluency that isn't secondhand — I build and prototype myself rather than directing others to translate ideas"*).

These are strong, repeated, absolute claims ("I don't hand off understanding to others," "rather than directing others to translate ideas") appearing across three separate executive pages — meaning they're positioned as the load-bearing narrative pillar of the whole site, not an incidental aside. The site substantiates this convincingly for frontend/product work: the mobile app was solo-built, Reqon and the AI Investment Manager are personal builds with real interactive recreations, and the AI Product Operating System case study is plausible as personal tooling. But the resume's own Acoustic bullets for the Snowflake/data-platform track all use directing verbs — "Led replacement," "Launched," "led migration," "Replaced... with" — not "built" or "wrote." That's a legitimate distinction (a Principal PM directing platform engineering work is normal and not something to apologize for), but it directly contradicts the absolute framing of "I don't hand off understanding to others... rather than directing others to translate ideas." A technical interviewer who cross-references the resume against the philosophy page will notice the data-platform bullets are exactly the "directing others" pattern the philosophy page disclaims. Either soften the absolute claim (e.g. "hands-on where it counts most" rather than a blanket "I don't hand off"), or be explicit that the hands-on claim applies specifically to product/frontend work while data-platform initiatives were led/directed — the current wording invites exactly the wrong reading.

### 6. Minor — SWOT "weaknesses" are the two safest, most PM-cliché deflections in the genre
`src/components/executive/swot/content.ts:23-27` — "can get pulled into technical details" and "have to consciously work at staying proactive." Not a technical-accuracy problem, but worth flagging since the persona is "an honest self-assessment, not a highlight reel" (per the page's own intro copy): both listed weaknesses are humble-brags a technical interviewer will recognize as the two most commonly rehearsed interview answers in the genre. Doesn't undermine any specific technical claim, but it slightly undercuts the credibility of the "honest, not a highlight reel" framing the page opens with — worth one genuinely limiting weakness (e.g., a technology/domain gap, or a scale of system Dustin hasn't operated at) if the goal is to survive a skeptical technical reader's radar for authenticity.

## Bottom line

Ship the mobile app, Product Catalog, and AI Product Operating System claims as-is — they're specific, checkable against the linked case studies, and would survive a technical follow-up. Before this goes in front of a technical audience, tighten bullets 2–4 (Astronomer/Snowflake orchestration, FinOps program, reconciliation platform) with at least one concrete mechanism each, fix the MCP claim to match what the case study actually says, and soften the "I don't hand off understanding to others" absolute so it doesn't contradict the resume's own "Led"/"Launched" verbs on the data-platform work.
